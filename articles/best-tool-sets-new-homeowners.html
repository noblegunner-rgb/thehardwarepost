Best Tool Sets for New Homeowners

Spring 2026

You just closed on a house. The inspector left a three-page list of minor things to address. Your dad says you need "a good set of tools." Your apartment toolkit — a butter knife, one hammer, and a IKEA Allen wrench collection — isn't going to cut it anymore.

Here's the thing about tool sets marketed to new homeowners: most of them include a lot of things you'll rarely use and skip the things you'll use constantly. We looked at four options and broke down exactly what you get and what you'll still need to buy.

What You Actually Need as a New Homeowner

Before we get to sets, here's the minimum inventory for real homeowner tasks (hanging pictures, tightening hinges, assembling furniture, basic plumbing fixes, patching drywall, mounting curtain rods):

  • Claw hammer (16 oz)
  • Tape measure (25 ft)
  • Phillips and flathead screwdrivers, multiple sizes
  • Adjustable wrench (8" or 10")
  • Needle-nose pliers
  • Combination pliers (slip-joint)
  • Utility knife
  • Level (24" minimum)
  • Drill/driver (not always in hand tool sets — important gap)
  • Stud finder

Use this list to evaluate every set below. The gaps tell you what to add to cart alongside it.

The Tool Sets

1. Stanley 210-Piece Mechanics Tool Set (STMT73795) — ~$89

Best for sheer variety on a tight budget — with a big asterisk.

Stanley's 210-piece set is everywhere, and for good reason: 210 tools for $89 is hard to argue with. You get a comprehensive socket set (both SAE and metric, 3/8" and 1/4" drive), combination wrenches, hex keys, a ratchet, and extensions — all in a hard plastic case that actually keeps things organized.

Here's the asterisk: this is a mechanics set, meaning it's optimized for working on cars and machinery, not houses. The socket-heavy inventory is great for tightening bolts on a lawnmower. It is not what you'll grab when you're hanging curtain rods or tightening a loose toilet seat. There's no hammer, no tape measure, no utility knife, no level, and no pliers beyond what's in the ratchet kit.

That said, the socket set is genuinely useful, and you'll eventually need it. If you buy this, budget another $40–$60 for the missing pieces: a hammer, tape measure, 6-in-1 screwdriver, and a level.

What's included: Ratchets (2), sockets (150+, SAE and metric, 1/4" and 3/8" drive), combination wrenches, hex keys, extensions
What's missing: Hammer, tape measure, level, pliers, utility knife, screwdrivers (standard)
Case: Hard plastic, blow-molded tray
Price: ~$89

2. DEWALT 168-Piece Home Tool Kit (DWMT45168) — ~$149

Best overall for genuine homeowner tasks. This is the one to buy.

DeWalt's 168-piece home tool kit is designed specifically for homeowners, not mechanics — and it shows. The set includes a 16 oz hammer, a 25-foot tape measure, a 6-in-1 screwdriver, combination pliers, needle-nose pliers, an 8" adjustable wrench, a utility knife, and a 9" torpedo level. In other words, it actually covers your daily-driver homeowner tasks.

The socket set is still solid — 3/8" and 1/4" drive ratchets, SAE and metric sockets — but it's balanced against the hand tools you need most. The carrying case is a soft bag rather than a hard case, which some people prefer (easier to haul around the house) and some don't (harder to keep organized).

DeWalt's quality is well above the typical "homeowner kit" level. The pliers have comfortable grips, the tape measure lock is solid, and the screwdrivers feel like actual tools rather than promotional giveaways. This is a set you'll still be using in 15 years.

The one gap: no stud finder. Add a basic stud finder (~$30) and you're fully kitted out for 95% of new homeowner tasks.

What's included: 16 oz hammer, 25 ft tape measure, 6-in-1 screwdriver, combination pliers, needle-nose pliers, 8" adjustable wrench, utility knife, 9" torpedo level, full socket set (3/8" and 1/4" drive, SAE and metric), hex keys, soft bag
What's missing: Stud finder, drill/driver
Case: Soft tool bag
Price: ~$149

3. Crescent CTK170CMP2 170-Piece Mechanics Tool Set — ~$129

Buy this if you want to prioritize socket/wrench quality over hand tools.

Crescent is a brand that doesn't get enough credit in the budget tool category. The 170-piece CTK170CMP2 is a mechanics-focused set, but the quality of the ratchets and sockets is noticeably better than Stanley's at the same price tier. The 72-tooth ratchets have a 5-degree swing arc — useful in tight spots. The chrome vanadium sockets feel more substantial than Stanley's comparable pieces.

Like the Stanley, this is mostly sockets and wrenches — not a homeowner hand-tool setup. You'll need to add a hammer, level, tape measure, and screwdrivers. But if your home has a lot of mechanical work (older house with lots of hardware, HVAC maintenance, working on a car in the garage), the Crescent's socket quality is worth the extra $40 over the Stanley.

The two-drawer blow-mold case is excellent. Everything has a spot. The trays are color-coded and clearly labeled. After 10 minutes with this set, you'll know where everything lives.

What's included: Ratchets (72-tooth, 1/4" and 3/8" drive), sockets (SAE and metric), combination wrenches (SAE and metric), hex keys, extensions
What's missing: Hammer, tape measure, level, pliers, screwdrivers (standard), utility knife
Case: Two-drawer blow-mold hard case
Price: ~$129

Crescent CTK170CMP2 on Lowe's

4. Black+Decker 20V MAX Drill/Driver + Hand Tool Kit (BDCDMT120IA) — ~$179

Best if you need a drill and don't have one — a crucial gap in every other set here.

Every other set in this guide leaves out a drill. The Black+Decker BDCDMT120IA combo kit fixes that. You get a 20V MAX lithium-ion drill/driver with a battery and charger, plus a 109-piece hand tool and accessory kit that includes a tape measure, levels, screwdrivers, pliers, and a basic bit set.

The drill itself is a basic homeowner-grade drill — 10 clutch positions, two-speed gearbox, 3/8" keyless chuck. It's not a DeWalt Pro or a Milwaukee M18, and it won't drill through concrete — but for assembling furniture, hanging shelves, and driving screws into wood, it does exactly what you need. The 20V MAX battery is compatible with other Black+Decker 20V tools, though the platform is smaller than Ryobi ONE+ or DeWalt 20V MAX.

The hand tool portion is competent but not exceptional. The tape measure is a 16-footer (shorter than ideal — you'll want a 25-footer for measuring rooms), and the sockets are basic. The real value is the drill + charger + battery in the same box.

If you already have a drill, skip this and buy the DeWalt 168-piece. If you don't have a drill and you're starting from zero, this gets you operational faster than anything else on this list.

What's included: 20V MAX drill/driver, battery, charger, tape measure (16 ft), screwdrivers, pliers, level, bit set, limited socket set
What's missing: Stud finder, 25 ft tape measure (the 16 ft is too short), hammer (check your kit — some versions include one)
Case: Soft bag + drill case
Price: ~$179

The Recommendation

Most new homeowners: DeWalt 168-piece DWMT45168. It's the only set here built around what you actually use in a house rather than in a shop. Add a stud finder and you're done.

If you don't have a drill: Black+Decker BDCDMT120IA. Yes, the hand tools are less complete — supplement with a $25 adjustable wrench and a 25-foot tape measure and you're in good shape.

If you're a car person too: Buy the DeWalt 168-piece for the house, and add the Stanley 210-piece socket set later when you need it for the car or lawnmower.

What to Buy Separately No Matter What

No kit ships with these, and you'll wish you had them:

  • A stud finder (~$30) — you'll use this the first week you're in the house
  • A good utility knife with extra blades (~$12)
  • A 48" level (~$25) — the torpedo level in kits is too short for hanging pictures in a straight line
  • A Milwaukee Tape Measure (25 ft) (~$20) — the best tape measure at any price
