Best Pressure Washers for Driveways (Not Contractors)

Spring 2026

Your driveway has a winter's worth of grime, oil stains, and whatever that mystery streak is near the garage door. You don't need a $2,000 trailer-mounted rig. You need a solid electric pressure washer that'll clean concrete without blowing through it, fits in a corner of the garage, and actually works when you pull it out once a year.

This guide is for homeowners with one or two concrete or pavers surfaces to clean. Not contractors. Not the guy pressure-washing houses for $300 a pop. You — with your driveway, patio, and deck.

What Actually Matters for Driveways

PSI (pounds per square inch) is pressure. GPM (gallons per minute) is flow. Multiply them to get Cleaning Units (CU = PSI × GPM), which is the real measure of cleaning power. A 2,000 PSI machine at 1.2 GPM has 2,400 CU. A 1,800 PSI machine at 1.8 GPM has 3,240 CU — and it'll clean faster.

For residential concrete driveways, target 1,800–2,300 PSI and 1.4+ GPM. You don't need more than that. Higher PSI with cheap internals usually means the motor burns out faster and the pump seals blow within two seasons.

Electric vs. gas: For a residential driveway under 2,000 sq ft, electric is fine. Gas washers are louder, require maintenance, and produce exhaust — none of which you need for spring cleanup. Gas makes sense if you're cleaning a long rural driveway or have heavy commercial grease to remove. Otherwise, plug it in.

Look for: a built-in detergent tank, a 25-foot hose minimum, a 35-foot power cord minimum, and a turbo/rotating nozzle in the box. If the turbo nozzle isn't included, add $20–$30 for one — it'll cut your driveway cleaning time in half.

The Pressure Washers

1. Sun Joe SPX3001 — 2,030 PSI | 1.76 GPM | ~$179

Best overall for most homeowners.

The Sun Joe SPX3001 is the sweet spot of residential electric pressure washers. It delivers 2,030 PSI and 1.76 GPM — that's 3,573 cleaning units, which is more than enough to strip a winter's worth of grime off concrete. It includes a 34-oz onboard detergent tank, five quick-connect nozzles (0°, 15°, 25°, 40°, and soap), and a 20-foot hose.

The 20-foot hose is the one real knock. For a long driveway, you'll want an extension. Sun Joe's extensions are compatible, and third-party M22 hoses work fine. The machine itself is 31 lbs with a roll cage — you can move it around a driveway without hauling it, which is more than you can say for a few competitors.

It's a 14.5-amp motor with a Total Stop System that shuts the pump off when you release the trigger. That extends pump life considerably — a common failure point in cheap machines is continuous-run pumps that stay pressurized even when you're not spraying.

Real-world cleaning: a standard two-car concrete driveway takes about 45–60 minutes with a 15° nozzle and a pass with the turbo nozzle (buy it separately — it's not included).

2. Ryobi RY142300 — 2,300 PSI | 1.2 GPM | ~$199

Buy this if you want a brand with real dealer support and a better warranty.

Ryobi's 2,300 PSI electric washer is a step up in durability from the Sun Joe. It runs a 13-amp motor with an axial cam pump — axial cam pumps are more durable than the wobble pumps in most cheap washers, and they hold pressure more consistently over the course of a long job.

The GPM is lower at 1.2, which puts cleaning units at 2,760 — below the Sun Joe. In practice, the higher PSI compensates on concrete. Concrete needs pressure more than volume. On a deck or siding, the Sun Joe's higher GPM wins. On a driveway, it's a wash (pun intended).

What Ryobi gets right: the 25-foot high-pressure hose, the included turbo nozzle, and Home Depot's return policy. If anything breaks in the first year, you can walk it back to HD — no shipping, no RMA. That peace of mind is worth something.

The onboard accessory storage is better than most competitors. The nozzles, spray gun, and hose all have dedicated spots on the machine. It's a small thing, but when you're not using it for 11 months of the year, keeping all the parts together matters.

PSI: 2,300 | GPM: 1.2 | Cleaning Units: ~2,760
Hose: 25 ft | Cord: 35 ft | Weight: 29 lbs
Detergent tank: Yes | Turbo nozzle: Included
Price: ~$199

3. Greenworks GPW2200 — 2,200 PSI | 1.8 GPM | ~$229

Best cleaning power for the money. Seriously underrated.

The Greenworks GPW2200 doesn't get talked about as much as Ryobi or Sun Joe, which is a shame, because 2,200 PSI at 1.8 GPM gives you 3,960 cleaning units — the highest in this guide. It'll do a two-car driveway in one pass without the feeling that you're babying it.

It has an axial pump (good), a 25-foot hose (good), a 35-foot power cord, and a 1-liter onboard soap tank. The turbo nozzle is included. At $229, you're paying more than the Sun Joe, but the GPM bump is real and the pump is more durable.

The main downside: Greenworks' 48V battery ecosystem doesn't connect to this corded unit, so if you're building out a Greenworks battery setup, this doesn't share batteries. It's a purely corded machine. For most people, that doesn't matter at all.

Weight is 34 lbs — slightly heavier than the Ryobi, still manageable. The wheels are larger than competitors, which helps when you're rolling across a cracked driveway.

Hose: 25 ft | Cord: 35 ft | Weight: 34 lbs
Detergent tank: Yes (1 L) | Turbo nozzle: Included
Price: ~$229

4. DeWalt DWPW2400 — 2,400 PSI | 1.1 GPM | ~$279

Best for occasional homeowners who want DeWalt build quality and nothing to go wrong.

DeWalt's corded electric pressure washer is a step up in build quality from the Sun Joe or Greenworks. The frame is sturdier, the connections are tighter, and it just feels like it was designed to not break. That matters when you're storing it for 11 months at a stretch and expecting it to work the next spring.

At 2,400 PSI and 1.1 GPM (2,640 CU), it's not the most powerful machine by cleaning units — the low GPM hurts it on paper. In practice, the pressure consistency is excellent. It doesn't surge or drop like cheaper machines.

It includes a turbo nozzle, a 25-foot hose, and a 35-foot cord. The soap applicator is a separate attachment, not an onboard tank — that's a slight inconvenience but also means you're not cleaning out a tank between uses.

At $279, you're paying for the name and the reliability. The extra $50–$80 over the Greenworks is only worth it if DeWalt build quality specifically matters to you, or if you've had cheaper machines die on you before.

Specs at a glance:
PSI: 2,400 | GPM: 1.1 | Cleaning Units: ~2,640
Hose: 25 ft | Cord: 35 ft | Weight: 32 lbs
Detergent tank: Soap attachment (not onboard) | Turbo nozzle: Included
Price: ~$279

5. Sun Joe SPX3500 — 2,300 PSI | 1.48 GPM | ~$239

Skip this — the SPX3001 is almost as capable for $60 less.

The SPX3500 is Sun Joe's step-up model with a brushless induction motor and higher PSI. It's a fine machine — 2,300 PSI at 1.48 GPM gives you 3,404 CU, a 20-foot hose, and a five-nozzle kit. The brushless motor is a genuine improvement over the SPX3001's universal motor.

But the price jump to $239 doesn't deliver proportionate cleaning power over the $179 SPX3001. The Greenworks GPW2200 at $229 beats it on cleaning units and includes a turbo nozzle. If you want to spend $239, get the Greenworks. If you want Sun Joe, get the SPX3001 and buy a $20 turbo nozzle separately. The SPX3500 lands in an awkward spot between two better options.

The Pick

Greenworks GPW2200 if you want the best cleaning power under $250. It has the highest CU in this roundup, includes a turbo nozzle, and has a 25-foot hose. The extra $50 over the Sun Joe SPX3001 is worth it for the GPM difference on a concrete driveway.

Sun Joe SPX3001 if you want to keep costs low. Add a $20 turbo nozzle and you have a complete setup for under $200 that handles a standard residential driveway without breaking a sweat.

Ryobi RY142300 if you want Home Depot's return policy and easy local warranty support. The 25-foot hose and included turbo nozzle are a plus.

One Thing to Buy Alongside Any of These

Get a surface cleaner attachment (~$30–$50). It's a circular spinning head that cleans large flat surfaces 4x faster than a regular nozzle and without the stripe pattern you get from sweeping back and forth. For a two-car driveway, it pays for itself in time saved on the first use.
